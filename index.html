Imbrace

Imbrace is a fashion tech alternative to encourage female millennials to embrace their low back pain and provide immediate ease without comprising with their sense of style and personality.

Most popular low back pain solutions are from the medical realm while some commercial alternatives focus on prevention rather than cure. Through all this, why should women compromise with their appearances or be made to feel different? How can a low back pain solution be made fashionable and make it blend in? Can fashion and medicine cross paths for a larger audience? Imbrace is an attempt to bridge these questions by making women accept this pain as part of themselves and do something about it.

Zip Back Jacket

Zip Back Jacket is a stylish wearable solution for Justin Moy, a sixteen year old teenager with muscular dystrophy and scoliosis. The jacket is multi-seasonal, warm and comfortable with the possibility of self-manipulation.

I was an engineering fellow, part of Team Justin, at Open Style Lab's summer 2016 program. As a result of his condition, Justin has neck, waist and arm contractions with limited range of motion. The focus was on a creative hoodie customized to his unique posture while reducing bunching and being easy to don and doff. In the process, we iterated sleeve patterns, jacket lengths, donning aesthetics and the use of fasteners and closures. The final jacket meets Justin's personality and style with at least three variations of draping it.

Plexus

Plexus is a collaborative effort that explores the Posthumanist notion of a technologically networked human body.

Like any other digital network, the human body is also a complex network, a neural network. Trying to understand the possibility of replacing a neural network with a technological network, Plexus is a speculative design piece questioning the human functionality beyond a single physical embodiment.

As a creative technologist on the team, I played a part in the ideation, fabrication and coding of the interactions. We worked with physical and digital fabrication tools and Internet of Things to achieve the desired interaction.

Book.it

Book.It focuses on the design of a user interface for readers and literary enthusiasts to exchange pre-owned books and literature related communications. The purpose was to have a clean, organized, intuitive and responsive layout for the user to navigate and understand the process without clutter.

With the extensive use of websites and apps on-the-go, layout designs for Book.it as a responsive website and as an independent app were created to show its potential in both media.

The Butterfly Effect

'The Butterfly Effect' is the theory that small causes have large effects.

Based on this 'chaos theory', 'The Butterfly Effect' is an interactive installation depicting the impact of brick building construction in San Francisco on it's butterfly population; in particular the 'Lange's Metalmark Butterfly'

The viewer is made to feel empathetic towards the demise of the specie and ponder about co-existence. The viewer's proximity to the installation changes the projection from a butterfly to a skull. The message conveyed is that a single step taken by them could have a greater impact on their surroundings.

BagLock

BagLock is a "smart" technology that lets you lock your backpack using a mobile app. It's seamless, smart, universal and invisible.

It is a proof-of-concept electromagnet controlled by a mobile app. The concept is about a lock that seals the bag from the inside, making it invisible. It is a way to avoid prying eyes and unwanted attention to the contents of your backpack in a crowded train or a public cafe, when the bag is beyond your visual peripheral.

Aurelia

Aurelia is a speculative installation piece setup as a 22nd century smart home device. The idea is set in a gelatinous era, where jellyfish are the sole source of power, food and information. With jellyfish at the center of the installation, their movement is controlled by mobile communication to induce generation of power from anywhere, home or away.

The installation is a combination of digital and physical elements and highlights possibilities in the world of connected objects. It is also an attempt to create awareness about natural sources that have the potential for becoming alternative sources for survival.

Drones in the Environment

Developed as a military experiment for warfare, drones are being widely commercialized with evolving technology. There is some acceptance towards drones in public spaces. At the same time, there are negative reactions as well.

'Drones in the Environment' is a collaborative research conducted to study people's reactions to drones. A drone in its manufactured body versus a 'visually friendly' cover were flown in public spaces to judge people's acceptance and fear levels. It was eventually realized that even though visuals determine perceptions, there are many other factors that play a role in acceptance of drones.
